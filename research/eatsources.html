Sources, Decisions and Meta-Analysis

The saying “You are what you eat” is quite literally true. Focusing on what we eat is increasingly recognized as one of the fundamental components of maintaining and improving overall health and well-being. The reasons for this are multifaceted and encompass both physical and mental health benefits.

Firstly, nutrition plays a critical role in maintaining optimal body function. The foods we consume are the primary source of nutrients, such as vitamins, minerals, and antioxidants, which our bodies need to function effectively. A balanced diet supports the immune system, promotes good heart health, maintains healthy blood pressure, and helps keep our organs working properly. Conversely, a diet lacking in essential nutrients can lead to a host of health problems, including weakened immune response, decreased energy levels, and a higher susceptibility to chronic diseases such as obesity, diabetes, heart disease, and some types of cancer.

Secondly, there's a strong link between diet and mental health. Research has shown that certain dietary patterns can affect the brain, influencing mood, energy levels, and cognitive functions. Foods high in omega-3 fatty acids, for example, are known to enhance brain function and are linked to a lower risk of depression. A diet rich in fruits, vegetables, lean protein, and whole grains can improve mental clarity, boost mood, and reduce the risk of mental health disorders. On the other hand, diets high in sugar and processed foods can exacerbate symptoms of anxiety and depression.

Moreover, what we eat also impacts our physical appearance, influencing skin health, weight, and overall physical fitness. Proper nutrition can help maintain healthy skin, promote weight management, and enhance muscular strength and endurance. This not only contributes to a better physical appearance but also boosts self-esteem and confidence.

Lastly, our food choices have far-reaching consequences beyond our personal health, impacting the environment and society at large. Choosing locally sourced, organic foods and reducing meat consumption can lower the carbon footprint associated with food production and support sustainable agricultural practices.

In conclusion, focusing on what we eat is essential for maintaining a healthy body, promoting mental well-being, achieving a positive self-image, enjoying a fulfilling relationship with food, and contributing to environmental sustainability. Making informed food choices is an investment in one's health and future, paving the way for a healthier and happier life.

Focusing on the quality of your food is paramount for maintaining overall health and well-being. One of the primary reasons to prioritize quality is the nutritional value that high-quality foods offer. These foods are typically rich in essential vitamins, minerals, and other nutrients crucial for optimal bodily function. By choosing nutrient-dense options, you can ensure that your body receives the necessary fuel it needs to thrive.

Moreover, the consumption of quality foods is associated with various health benefits. A diet consisting of high-quality ingredients can significantly reduce the risk of chronic diseases such as obesity, heart disease, and diabetes. These foods often contain fewer additives, preservatives, and unhealthy fats commonly found in processed options, thereby promoting better long-term health outcomes.

In addition to its health benefits, prioritizing quality food can have a positive impact on energy levels and weight management. Nutrient-dense foods help maintain stable energy levels throughout the day, preventing the energy crashes associated with consuming processed or sugary snacks. Furthermore, these foods are typically lower in calories and higher in fiber, which can help control appetite and promote satiety, aiding in weight management efforts.

The quality of your food also plays a crucial role in digestive health. High-quality foods are generally easier to digest and less likely to cause digestive discomfort, such as bloating or indigestion. By choosing whole, minimally processed foods, you can support a healthy gut microbiome and maintain optimal digestive function.

Beyond individual health benefits, prioritizing quality food choices can have positive implications for the environment. Opting for sustainably sourced, ethically produced foods supports environmentally friendly farming practices and reduces the carbon footprint associated with food production. By making informed choices about the quality of the food we consume, we can contribute to both personal well-being and environmental sustainability.

Having a healthy body weight typically means having a body mass index (BMI) within the recommended range for your height and age. However, it's important to note that BMI is just one measure of healthy weight and doesn't account for factors such as muscle mass, bone density, and overall body composition. Therefore, other indicators such as waist circumference, height, body fat percentage, and overall physical fitness should also be considered when assessing health status. Additionally, maintaining a healthy weight involves a balanced diet, regular physical activity, adequate sleep, and managing stress effectively.

That’s why our calorie Calculator is more than just BMI. It uses BMI with an offset for your height as BMI is mainly focused on “average” sized individuals. So we also use BMR and TDEE. BMR represents the number of calories your body needs to maintain basic physiological functions while at rest, such as breathing, circulating blood, and maintaining body temperature. It's essentially the number of calories your body would burn if you were to lie in bed all day without moving. BMR is influenced by factors such as age, gender, body composition, and genetics. Calculating your BMR can help determine the baseline number of calories your body needs to function.

TDEE accounts for all the calories your body burns in a day, including physical activity on top of your BMR. It encompasses the calories burned through activities such as exercise, walking, standing, and even non-exercise activities like fidgeting and maintaining posture. TDEE is influenced by physical activity level, occupation, lifestyle, and exercise habits. To maintain your current weight, your calorie intake should roughly match your TDEE. If you consume more calories than your TDEE, you'll likely gain weight, and if you consume fewer calories, you'll likely lose weight.

In combining a modified BMI based on standard deviations, BMR, and TDEE our calculator gives you a very realistic idea of how many calories an individual should aim for in a day. This does not mean that it will be “perfect” for every person and diet changes should always be discussed with your doctor or a medical professional.

Maintaining a healthy body weight is crucial for overall well-being and reducing the risk of various health problems. Here's why it's important and what it means:

  • Reduced Risk of Chronic Diseases: Being at a healthy weight lowers the risk of developing chronic diseases such as heart disease, type 2 diabetes, high blood pressure, and certain types of cancer.
  • Nutritional Deficiencies: Underweight individuals may need to consume more nutrients, which can lead to deficiencies in vitamins, minerals, and essential nutrients. This can weaken the immune system, impair wound healing, and increase the risk of infections.
  • Improved Mobility and Functionality: Carrying excess weight can strain the body's joints and organs, leading to decreased mobility and functionality. Maintaining a healthy weight allows for better movement and physical performance.
  • Enhanced Mental Health: There's a strong correlation between physical health and mental well-being. Maintaining a healthy weight can improve self-esteem, body image, and overall mental health.
  • Better Quality of Life: Being at a healthy weight promotes better energy levels, sleep quality, and overall quality of life. It allows individuals to engage in daily activities without experiencing limitations due to weight-related issues.
  • Longevity: Studies have consistently shown that individuals who maintain a healthy weight tend to live longer and have a higher quality of life in their later years.

Our food philosophy is aimed at helping individuals get their target micronutrients and macronutrients every day while also feeling satiated. Macronutrients consist of carbohydrates, proteins, and fats. Micronutrients consist of vitamins and minerals. Below is a quick overview of each:

Macronutrients:

  • Carbohydrates: Carbs are the body's primary source of energy. They provide fuel for daily activities and are especially important for high-intensity exercise. Sources include grains, fruits, vegetables, and legumes.
  • Proteins: Proteins are crucial for building and repairing tissues, synthesizing enzymes and hormones, and supporting immune function. They also play a role in maintaining muscle mass and bone density. Good sources of protein include meat, fish, poultry, eggs, dairy products, legumes, nuts, and seeds.
  • Fats: Dietary fats are essential for providing energy, absorbing fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, and K), maintaining cell structure, and supporting brain health. Healthy fat sources include avocados, nuts, seeds, olive oil, fatty fish, and coconut oil.

Micronutrients:

  • Vitamins: Vitamins are essential for various bodily functions, including immune function, metabolism, vision, and bone health. They act as coenzymes, assisting enzymes in facilitating chemical reactions in the body. Examples include vitamin A, vitamin C, vitamin D, vitamin E, and the B vitamins (such as B12, folate, and riboflavin). These vitamins are found in a wide range of foods, including fruits, vegetables, whole grains, dairy products, and meats.
  • Minerals: Minerals are important for bone health, muscle function, fluid balance, and nerve transmission. They also play a role in enzyme function and hormone regulation. Examples include calcium, magnesium, potassium, sodium, iron, zinc, and selenium. Minerals are found in various foods, including dairy products, leafy greens, nuts, seeds, legumes, whole grains, and lean meats.

Both macronutrients and micronutrients are essential for optimal health. A well-balanced diet that includes a variety of foods from all food groups ensures that you're getting an adequate intake of both macro and micronutrients. Deficiencies in either category can lead to health problems, so it's important to pay attention to your overall dietary intake and make choices that promote nutrient diversity and adequacy.

While meeting all your body's needs for eating is great, we also need to consider our impact on the world around us. This adds an entire layer of complexity to our determining what we eat and even where we can purchase it. In this section, we are going to explore some of the most common discussions on ethics in the food industry: Consumer Responsibility, Environmental Impact, Human Welfare & Social Justice, Animal Welfare, and Genetic Modifications. It is very important to note that making 100 percent ethical choices may be extremely difficult verging on impossible for some individuals, the reason may vary from allergies to food availability.

First, understanding consumer responsibility is key before analyzing other ethical food decisions. It’s important to understand whether worrying about our decisions is even worthwhile or something we should take ownership of. There is an ongoing debate about the extent of individual versus collective responsibility in making ethical food choices. This includes discussions about whether it is enough for consumers to change their buying habits, or whether there should be larger systemic changes to food production and distribution systems.

For addressing this first part we may want to simply focus on what we can control (commonly discussed in Stoicism). Emphasize focusing on our own actions and decisions rather than getting overly concerned with factors outside our control. In the context of consumer responsibility, this could mean making ethical and sustainable food choices whenever possible, recognizing that one's control might be limited by factors like availability, cost, and personal circumstances.

Borrowing from contractualism, which is based on the principle that actions are morally right or wrong based on whether they can be justified to others, especially the affected party. Applying this ethical theory (as described by T.M. Scanlon) to consumer responsibility, one could argue that consumers should make choices that they could justify to others on reasonable grounds. This means considering the impacts of their choices on all affected parties and ensuring that their actions do not contribute to systems of harm or injustice. Scanlon might suggest that consumers have a responsibility to avoid contributing to harm through their purchasing decisions, such as harm caused by environmentally destructive practices, animal cruelty, or unfair labor practices. Consumers should consider the broader implications of their food choices and whether they could defend these choices to those who are adversely affected by them.

While Immanuel Kant's ethical philosophy which is grounded in deontology and emphasizes the importance of duty and the inherent value of individuals may go as far as to argue that consumers have a moral duty to make ethical food choices. Based on Kant’s principle of treating humanity, whether in one's own person or in that of another, always as an end and never merely as a means to an end. In the context of food, this could mean considering the impact of one's food choices on the well-being of animals, workers, and the environment.

Even if we consider philosophies that prioritize individual desires, needs, and interests over those of others or the common good we only see short-term gain from our food practices, and shallow gains at that. If an egoist prioritizes immediate self-gratification and benefits, they might disregard broader ethical considerations related to food consumption. They could prioritize convenience, taste, and cost over ethical factors. However, even an intelligent egoist would want long-term gains. They might recognize that sustainable and ethical consumption practices contribute to a healthier environment, a more stable society, and ultimately their own well-being. From this viewpoint, an egoist might choose to consume ethically produced food, reduce waste, and support fair labor practices, not primarily out of concern for others or the environment, but because they believe these actions will promote a more desirable world for themselves.

So if we have to consider the impact we have on the world around us, what might we affect? Let’s consider the impact we have on the environment. The environmental ethics of food production involve issues like deforestation, carbon emissions, water use, and biodiversity loss. Many of these issues have very close ties to Humane and Animal Welfare issues. Critics of large-scale industrial farming argue that it is unsustainable and harmful to the planet, advocating for local, organic, or plant-based diets as more sustainable options.

Consider terms such as “organic”. Organic refers to the way agricultural products are grown and processed. In the context of food and farming, organic production is generally defined by practices that rely on ecological processes, biodiversity, and cycles adapted to local conditions, rather than the use of synthetic inputs like chemical fertilizers, pesticides, and genetically modified organisms (GMOs).

For a product to be labeled as "organic," it must meet specific standards set by a country or a group of countries. In the United States, for instance, the USDA National Organic Program (NOP) is responsible for setting these standards and certifying products as organic. The organic standards cover various aspects, including soil quality, animal raising practices, pest and weed control, and the use of additives.

Whether you can trust the organic label depends largely on the integrity and enforcement of these standards by the certifying bodies. In countries with strict organic certification processes, such as the United States, Canada, and the European Union, the organic label is generally trustworthy. These regions have established clear standards and regular inspection protocols to ensure compliance. However, the reliability of the label can vary by country and region, depending on the strictness of the regulations and the oversight of the certifying bodies.

Consumers looking to buy organic products should look for official organic seals or certifications on the packaging, which indicate that the product meets the standards set by the certifying organization. It's also beneficial to be aware of the specific organic standards and practices in your region to better understand what the organic label signifies for products you purchase.

From this brief overview of the term organic you can see questioning this deeply about every food item and every certification can become a full-time job, which is why we created a list of certifications to look for when purchasing your foods. On top of this, our food philosophies focus on whole foods already cuts out a lot of bad industry practices. Buying directly from the source gives you more control.

While funding the wrong parts of the food industry hurts us all, it can have a much larger impact on specific individuals. Human Welfare includes concerns about the fair treatment and fair pay of workers within the food industry, from farming to retail. It also covers issues of localized health concerns caused by air pollution, water contamination, odor nuisances and noise pollution. Addressing these problems and health concerns requires comprehensive regulatory measures, community engagement, and industry accountability but that doesn’t mean you can’t help.

Both vegetable and animal industries are large contributors to these issues. However, this is why the choice to be plant-based (some meat), vegetarian, or vegan while shopping organic can make a difference. Fewer farm animals mean fewer of all these problems. While vegetable farms are responsible for pesticides and deforestation, you can buy organic leaving the only problem to be deforestation. Sometimes minimizing our impact is the best we can do.

If you aren’t ready or physically can’t be vegetarian or vegan, that doesn’t mean you can’t have a huge impact on the food industry too. Consider buying meats with certifications such as the Demeter Biodynamic certification. This certification requires adherence to biodynamic farming principles that view the farm as a self-sustaining ecosystem. Animal welfare is a key component, and the certification ensures animals are treated with respect and care. Eating less meat and more than just one cut type (such as chicken breast, thigh, wing, drumstick or full body) can allow the industry to provide animals with longer fuller lives, which in turns reduces the amount of air pollution, water contamination, odor nuisances and noise pollution.

Now this brings us to our discussion on animal welfare. This involves concerns about the treatment of animals in the food production process. Arguments against certain practices include objections to factory farming, animal testing, and inhumane slaughter techniques. Advocates for animal welfare often promote veganism or vegetarianism as more ethical dietary choices or at least urge for more humane farming practices.

Animal rights are predicated on the recognition that animals, like humans, are capable of feeling pain, experiencing emotions, and having interests that deserve consideration and respect. This ethical stance is rooted in the principle of equal consideration of interests, a cornerstone of moral philosophy. According to philosopher Peter Singer, this principle requires us to give equal weight to the interests of all beings capable of suffering, regardless of their species. This viewpoint challenges the traditional anthropocentric ethical viewpoint, which places human interests above those of other animals.

One of the main arguments for animal rights is based on the capacity of animals to suffer. Scientific research has consistently shown that many animals are sentient beings with complex emotional lives. They can feel pain, suffer, and experience joy, much like humans. From a logical standpoint, if causing unnecessary suffering is wrong when it comes to humans, consistency demands that we extend this moral consideration to other sentient beings. The arbitrary line between humans and other animals, known as "speciesism," is increasingly seen as an unjustifiable form of discrimination.

As mentioned earlier we should consider contractualism. If the affected party were these animals that we cannot discriminate against because of their species, we would determine the decision to eat them as unethical. However, ethics are not always black and white with yes or no answers. Though it may not seem ideal, it does appear that humans have evolved to eat at least some meat. While technology around imitation meats continues to grow others still may choose to eat animal meat for a variety of reasons. While not as morally justifiable as veganism, ethical plant-based eating can be achieved and provide a world of difference in animal welfare. Mind Body Minimalism tools will help those who continue to eat meat find the most ethical path forward.

Lastly, we will discuss our thoughts on genetic modification and biotechnology: The ethics of using biotechnology in food production, including genetically modified organisms (GMOs), is a hotly debated topic. Supporters argue that such technologies can lead to more resilient and abundant food supplies, while critics raise concerns about long-term environmental impacts, health risks, and corporate control over the food supply.

One of the primary advantages of GMOs is their potential to increase food security. By modifying crops to resist pests and disease, yields can be significantly increased, which is particularly important in areas struggling with food scarcity. Additionally, GMOs can be engineered to withstand harsh environmental conditions like drought or poor soil quality, potentially turning previously unarable land into productive farmland. From a philosophical perspective, proponents argue that using technology to alleviate hunger aligns with ethical principles emphasizing the reduction of suffering and promotion of human welfare.

Furthermore, GMOs can contribute to environmental sustainability. For instance, crops engineered to require less fertilizer or pesticide reduce the need for these chemicals, which can have harmful effects on ecosystems. This could be seen as adhering to the ethical principle of stewardship, where humans have a responsibility to protect and preserve the natural world for future generations.

However, there are significant concerns associated with GMOs and biotechnology. One of the main issues is the potential for unintended environmental impacts. For example, GMO crops could cross-breed with wild relatives, potentially leading to loss of biodiversity. There's also the risk of creating superweeds or pest strains resistant to current control methods, which could lead to greater use of chemicals, not less. This raises ethical concerns related to the precautionary principle, which suggests that new technologies should be approached with caution until their effects are fully understood.

From a socio-economic standpoint, there's worry that GMOs can lead to increased inequality. Patents on genetically modified seeds can concentrate power in the hands of a few large corporations, limiting the autonomy of small-scale farmers and possibly leading to increased costs. This scenario raises ethical questions about justice and fairness, particularly the rights of farmers versus the rights of companies to profit from their innovations.

Ultimately there is no way to make an entirely ethical choice here unless you solely eat from your organically grown community/personal garden. While GMOs offer considerable benefits in terms of food security and environmental sustainability, they also pose ethical, environmental, and socio-economic challenges. The debate over their use is complex, involving a careful balancing of competing interests and values. Mind Body Minimalism will continue to keep its eyes on this topic in hopes of a clearer answer. After all, we must also evolve and continue to grow as new information is gained.
